Pendjari is roughly the size of a small country, sits on the border with Burkina Faso and Niger, and contains one of the last viable populations of West African lions on earth. It is not the Serengeti. The animals are harder to find. The roads will rattle your back teeth. You will not see another tour vehicle for hours at a stretch.

That last bit is the whole point.

## The mornings

We leave the lodge at 5:30. The air is cool, the savanna is still mostly grey, and the camp's two dogs come with us as far as the perimeter gate. After that it's just the truck, the ranger, and a coffee thermos.

![Pre-dawn departure from the eco-lodge perimeter](/images/1535941339077-2dd1c7963098.jpg
The first half-hour is mostly listening. You learn to hear what the ranger hears — the alarm calls of baboons two ridges over, the *kwock* of guineafowl that means something is in the grass.

## What we saw

In five mornings:

- A pride of seven lions, including two cubs, at a waterhole on day three.
- A herd of about forty elephants crossing the river at dusk on day four — the longest fifteen minutes I've ever spent holding a long lens.
- Roan antelope, hartebeest, oribi, waterbuck, baboon, vervet, warthog, and (briefly) a cheetah at the very edge of visual range.
- Hippos, every day. There are a lot of hippos.

![Elephant herd at the river](/images/1456926631375-92c8ce872def.jpg
What we *didn't* see: another vehicle, except on the entry road. Not once during the actual game drives.

<PullQuote>
Pendjari is not better than East Africa. It is different. The animals are fewer and farther between. The reward is that you'll be alone with them.
</PullQuote>

## The birding is bonkers

Don't sleep on the birds. Pendjari has somewhere north of 470 recorded species, and the river segment is one of the great unsung birding sites in West Africa. Bring binoculars. Bring a guide who can actually call them.

## Practical notes

If you're planning a Pendjari trip:

- **Dry season only.** Roughly December to April. The park essentially closes in the wet months — roads become impassable.
- **Internal flight to Natitingou** saves you a brutal 8-hour drive from Cotonou. Worth it.
- **Stay inside the park** if you can — the eco-lodge has 16 rooms and books out in peak weeks.
- **Bring a real camera.** Phones won't reach. A 200–400mm zoom is the minimum useful focal length for game drives.

If you want to do this trip, we run it from January through mid-April. Five days, small group, fully scoped: see [Pendjari: West Africa's Last Wild Heart](/tours/pendjari-safari).
