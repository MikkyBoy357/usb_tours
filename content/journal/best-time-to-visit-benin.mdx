The short answer: **mid-November through mid-February** is the sweet spot — cool, dry, and the parks are open. But "the best time" depends on what you've come for. Here is the honest version, month by month.

## The seasons, simply

Benin has roughly two climates stacked top-to-bottom:

- **The south** (Cotonou, Ouidah, Ganvié, Abomey) — coastal, humid year-round. Two rainy seasons: a big one from April to July, a short one in October.
- **The north** (Natitingou, Tata Somba, Pendjari) — drier overall, with one long wet season from May to September.

Tourism essentially follows the dry seasons. The wet months aren't unpleasant in the south — but in the north, dirt roads become impassable and the park closes.

## Month by month

### November — December

The first dry, cool weeks. Air clears, dust hasn't arrived yet, north and south are both glorious. Pendjari opens for game drives. Voodoo Festival happens on **January 10**, but the country starts gearing up for it before Christmas. **This is the best window.**

### January

Peak season. The Voodoo Festival on January 10 is the single biggest cultural event in the country — thousands of pilgrims converge on Ouidah, processions, music, public ceremonies. If you've ever wanted to witness Vodun in its full, public, joyful form, plan around this.

Book early. Hotels in Ouidah fill up six months out.

### February

Still glorious in the south. In the north, the **Harmattan** — a dry, dusty wind off the Sahara — peaks. Skies turn grey-pink, sunsets are unreal, but distance shots get hazy. Trade-off.

### March — April

The shoulder. Hot, dry, still good for safari but the heat is real (38°C / 100°F daytime in Pendjari is normal). The country empties of foreign travelers, prices drop. Worth considering if you're heat-tolerant and crowd-averse.

### May — September

The rains. Lush, green, beautiful. The south is fine to visit, the food markets are at their best, beaches are quieter. But Pendjari is closed and northern roads are unreliable. If you only have one shot at Benin, save it for the dry season.

### October

The short rains end. The country is still wet underfoot but clearing. Pendjari isn't quite open yet. Cotonou is at its greenest. A quiet, undervalued month.

## Festivals worth planning around

| Festival | When | Where | What |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Voodoo Festival** | January 10 | Ouidah | National holiday. Processions, ceremonies, music. The country's biggest event. |
| **Gelede** | March–May (variable) | Yoruba villages near border | UNESCO-listed masked dances honoring women's spiritual power. |
| **Egungun** | Variable, mostly dry season | Across the south | Ancestor masquerade processions. Public and private versions. |
| **Nonvitcha** | Whit Monday (May/June) | Grand-Popo | Coastal Mina people's annual gathering — food, dance, family. |

## Weather, in plain numbers

Daily highs in Cotonou (south):

- **Nov–Feb:** 30–32°C / 86–90°F. Humid but tolerable.
- **Mar–May:** 32–34°C / 90–93°F. Hot, building toward rains.
- **Jun–Sep:** 28–30°C / 82–86°F. Wet.
- **Oct:** 30–31°C / 86–88°F. Drying out.

Daily highs in Natitingou (north):

- **Nov–Feb:** 32–36°C / 90–97°F days, **15–20°C / 60–68°F nights**. Bring a layer.
- **Mar–May:** 36–40°C / 97–104°F. Genuinely hot.
- **Jun–Sep:** 28–32°C / 82–90°F. Wet.
